Overview

Recent multimodal large language models can describe visible objects and actions, but they still struggle to recover the deeper creative logic behind professional video production. Existing benchmarks largely treat videos as temporal sequences of frames, which leaves major gaps in narrative reasoning, cinematographic understanding, editing analysis, and audio-visual coordination.

CUBench addresses this gap by reverse-engineering the full creative lifecycle of video production. It evaluates models across creativity, plot understanding, script reconstruction, intra-shot expression, inter-shot expression, and music, using a hybrid evaluation pipeline that combines expert-calibrated judging for abstract semantics with deterministic matching for objective cinematic grammar.

Abstract

While recent Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) excel at surface-level video perception, they fundamentally struggle to interpret the complex creative intents and artistic synthesis inherent in professional video production. Existing benchmarks predominantly treat videos as shallow temporal sequences, ignoring sophisticated macro-level narrative intents and micro-level cinematographic techniques. To bridge this critical gap, we introduce the Creative Understanding Benchmark (CUBench), the first comprehensive omni-modal evaluation framework that assesses MLLMs by reverse-engineering the full-lifecycle video creation process.

CUBench systematically deconstructs video comprehension into six core industrial dimensions that mirror professional film production roles, spanning 29 fine-grained sub-dimensions. We curate a high-quality dataset of 1,122 full videos and 831 cinematic clips, rigorously annotated by domain experts. Extensive benchmarking reveals severe shortcomings in state-of-the-art MLLMs regarding implicit narrative reasoning and cinematic grammar, with the leading model scoring only 55.94%.

Benchmark Design

Six industrial perspectives

Director, Screenwriter, Script Supervisor, Cinematographer, Editor, and Composer jointly define the benchmark scope.

Macro to micro evaluation

Full-video evaluation captures global creative intent, while cinematic clips isolate local shot language and editing techniques.

Hybrid evaluation protocol

Expert-calibrated LLM judging handles open-ended semantics, and objective matching measures deterministic visual grammar.

Creativity

Creative hook, strategic intent, and target audience.

Plot Understanding

Synopsis, genre, narrative structure, character, theme, pacing, and emotional tone.

Script Reconstruction

Rebuilding structured scripts from dialogues, expressions, effects, and timelines.

Intra-shot Expression

Angle, framing, movement, lighting, shot size, and descriptive shot interpretation.

Inter-shot Expression

Cut logic, transitions, pacing, and visual continuity across adjacent shots.

Music

Genre, tempo, tone, and music atmosphere aligned with visual storytelling.

Figures and Analysis

The benchmark spans six primary industrial dimensions and 29 fine-grained sub-dimensions.

CUBench includes short, medium, long, and ultra-long videos to stress-test context reasoning.

Sub-dimension results reveal large gaps between explicit cue extraction and deeper creative reasoning.

Models often confuse editing techniques because they capture surface visual shifts but miss narrative intent.

Low-scoring cases are dominated by semantic misinterpretation, hallucination, and missing key information.

Main Findings

Current MLLMs remain far from reliable creative co-pilots. Even the top-performing model, Gemini-2.5-Pro, reaches only 55.94% overall on CUBench.

Implicit narrative reasoning is still the major bottleneck. Models can often summarize dialogue or recognize settings, but struggle to infer character relations, deep script logic, and creative intent.

Cinematic grammar is not truly understood. In cut and shot analysis, models often rely on superficial visual shortcuts instead of reasoning about temporal and narrative structure.

Audio-visual synergy remains difficult. Music understanding scores are modest, indicating that current multimodal systems still have limited ability to align soundtrack cues with visual storytelling.
